Data Science Project

UK education and its outcome

Aims: In economics, several papers have researched the impacts of education on students' outcomes. Their purpose is to implement better educational policies by studying education both theoretically and empirically. They focus on not only students' attainment but also unequal benefits from education. This project will explain how the education system works in the UK and its effect on short-run and long-run outcomes.

In England, the allocation of public schools is determined by the local authority based on the preferences of parents and children. However, if there is an oversubscription, the schools decide who is offered a place according to some criteria. In general, schools are not allowed to select based on student ability, but there are a few local authorities where it is still allowed.

The graph below illustrates how the allocations differ between selective schools and non-selective schools. For the ratio of children who are offered to their most preferable school, non-selective schools are larger than selective schools. However, for the second and third preferences, selective schools are larger than non-selective schools. This is partly because selective schools tend to be oversubscribed.

The data I used for this graph is from GOV.UK education statistics. (here) Although the data on the website is already clean, the challenge was to use the data which has three dimensions; preference, time, and selectivity. To combine the graphs and make them easily comparable, I used the "hconcut" tool to visualize.

The next graph indicates the female/male ratio for each admission type from 2018-2022. In selective schools, the female-student ratio is higher than in non-selective schools on average. The data I use is again from GOV.UK here. Since the ratio is not provided directly from data, I used Python to aggregate the number of pupils grouped by gender, admission types, and time period. Then I calculated the ratio for each year and admission types. The Python code I used is here. The hardest challenge to visualize was to draw the vertical line which represents the average ratio. I found mark type "rule" worked in this case.

Non-selective 1 indicates the non-selective schools in highly selective areas. Non-selective 2 means the non-selective schools in other areas.

The graph below shows the average Attainment 8 scores by admission type. This clearly shows the students in selective schools got higher scores than students in non-selective schools. However, because the pupils in selective schools have a higher ability to study before they enter, the value added by schools is still unclear. This data is on the GOV.UK (here).

The next graph is a scatter plot of the average progression score and higher education progression rate by local authorities with its regression line. The progression score is a value-added score for each pupil. There is a clear trend that the progression rate and progression score are highly correlated by local authority level. The Data is from GOV.UK here. To make the scatter plots, I aggregate the progression score and progression rate using Python. The difficulty was to aggregate two different variables. To tackle this, I first aggregate the progression score and progression rate separately, and merge them by the local authority name. The Python code is here.

Also, the map below indicates the average progression score by local authorities. Although there are many missing values, the local authorities in London have a higher progression score. The data I used is here.

The next graph shows the wage per hour by education level. According to the graph, highly educated people tend to earn higher wages. The data is here.

Finally, I plotted the government expenditure on education. It shows the expenditure has decreased over the last two decades. To make this graph, I used the World Bank API here. The challenge in making this graph was to find the format of the World Bank API. Also, by just using the URL, the graph does not work, so I make use of the "format" tool.

Conclusion: In the current UK education system, pupils' characteristics such as gender are different among admission types. Also, the attainment of education is heterogeneous among regions and school types. The difference in the attainment will lead to the difference in higher education progression rates, and ultimately affect future income. Although one of the purposes of education is to increase students' abilities, looking at the distribution of gains from education is also important. Therefore, the government needs to implement educational policies to enhance students' attainment and to make the equal distribution of gains from education. However, the government expenditure on education has decreased recently. It is quite difficult to judge whether the reduction is valid or not because the effect of education is often hard to measure. Thus, more advanced methods are needed to correctly estimate the impacts of education on outcomes.
